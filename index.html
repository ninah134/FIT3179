Climate Change

Global Challenges and Action

Location

The world map visualises global greenhouse gas emissions, highlighting countries with the highest CO₂ output in 2025. Larger circles represent greater emission volumes, with the most significant emitters concentrated in Asia, North America, and Europe. China stands out as the largest emitter worldwide, reflecting its massive industrial output and energy demands, followed by the United States. Australia, while contributing a smaller share globally, remains one of the highest per-capita emitters due to its reliance on fossil fuels and resource-intensive industries. This distribution shows how industrialised regions contribute disproportionately to global emissions, reflecting ongoing challenges in achieving climate balance and equitable emission reductions.

As rising carbon emissions reveal the uneven environmental footprint across regions, the next step is to understand how patterns of global energy consumption drive these disparities.

Energy Consumption

Globally, energy consumption varies widely by region, reflecting differences in industrial development, population size, and access to renewable resources. Asia remains the largest energy consumer, driven by rapid industrialisation and urban growth, while North America and Europe maintain high but stabilising energy demands as they transition toward cleaner technologies. In contrast, parts of Oceania consume far less energy overall, though their renewable adoption rates are gradually increasing.

The trellis chart provides a regional comparison of fuel emission trends across multiple countries, illustrating how these patterns align with global energy use and can contribute to global warming. Each small multiple highlights geographic differences, showing that emerging economies are steadily increasing fuel emissions, while developed nations show slower growth or slight declines. Coal consumption remains highest in China, driven by its vast industrial production and energy demand, whereas the United States records the greatest use of natural gas and oil, reflecting its diversified yet carbon-intensive energy mix. In contrast, Australia exhibits one of the lowest levels of coal, natural gas, and oil consumption among the group, indicating a positive shift toward cleaner energy sources and stronger climate action efforts.

As energy drives economies, it also fuels the rising temperatures that define
our century’s greatest climate challenge.

Temperature

Global surface temperatures continue to rise in the current century, reflecting the ongoing intensification of climate change worldwide. These visuals compare temperature changes and anomalies across countries and time periods, revealing regional disparities in climate patterns. Russia and Saudi Arabia record the highest surface temperature changes with 2.80°C increases, reflecting their strong link to greenhouse gas emissions and extensive reliance on fossil fuel industries. Australia shows a moderate increase in temperature, aligning more closely with the global average and demonstrating gradual progress toward sustainable climate practices. Meanwhile, European countries exhibit the lowest temperature changes, indicating effective climate care and stronger environmental policies.

The accompanying humidity chart further supports these observations. Russia records the highest humidity levels, correlating with its status as one of the world’s largest greenhouse gas emitters and underscoring the intensifying impacts of climate change. In comparison, Australia experiences slightly higher humidity than the United States, reflecting differences in regional weather patterns and coastal influences, yet both countries continue to face challenges in balancing emission control with climate resilience.

As rising temperatures reshape weather patterns and melt polar ice, their silent
consequence begins to surface — the steady rise of our global seas.

Sea Level

Sea level rise data shows increasing rates of change globally. The heatmaps and line chart below illustrate global variability across the years and the overall accelerating trend. Higher sea levels are typically observed during the spring and summer months, when warmer ocean temperatures cause thermal expansion of water, amplifying short-term rises. Over the years, the global mean sea level has steadily increased, reaching approximately 56.20 mm in December 2019 — one of the highest recorded levels to date. This rise reflects the cumulative impact of melting glaciers, ice sheets, and thermal expansion caused by global warming. Such acceleration poses a major threat to coastal ecosystems, low-lying regions, and human livelihoods worldwide. Continued increases in sea level heighten risks of flooding, erosion, and the loss of habitable land, reinforcing the urgent need for global climate action and sustainable adaptation strategies.

As oceans swell and coastlines shift, the atmosphere responds in kind —
redistributing moisture, reshaping rainfall, and redefining the world’s
precipitation patterns.

Precipitation

Changes in precipitation patterns reveal regional disparities — some areas experience increased rainfall, while others face prolonged dry seasons. These shifts have major implications for agriculture, ecosystems, and climate stability. The regions experiencing the wettest rainfall levels include Australia, the United States, Denmark, and the French Southern Territories, where increased moisture contributes to lush vegetation and stronger hydrological cycles. In contrast, the driest regions — including Mauritania, Canada, Libya, and San Marino — face reduced rainfall, which intensifies drought conditions and water scarcity.

From a scientific perspective, precipitation plays a crucial role in regulating Earth’s climate system. When rainfall increases in certain areas, it can enhance carbon absorption by forests and soils, helping to offset some greenhouse gas emissions. However, excessive rainfall and storms can lead to flooding, soil erosion, and ecosystem disruption, worsening the effects of climate change. On the other hand, declining precipitation and prolonged dryness reduce soil moisture, diminish crop yields, and increase the likelihood of wildfires, releasing more carbon dioxide into the atmosphere. Thus, precipitation can act as both a mitigating and worsening factor for climate change, depending on its balance across regions.

As rain nourishes the earth and shapes the rhythm of life, it feeds the forests that stand as our planet’s greatest guardians — storing carbon, sheltering biodiversity, and balancing the climate we depend on.

Forest Coverage

Forest coverage is a critical factor in carbon storage and biodiversity preservation. These charts explore deforestation trends and variations in green cover by region, emphasising the vital role of forests in mitigating climate change. Asia records the highest air quality index, which is a negative indicator of heavy pollution levels. This aligns with the fact that China and other Asian nations are among the largest greenhouse gas emitters, corresponding with their relatively low EV adoption and energy efficiency scores.

In contrast, Europe demonstrates high EV adoption and strong energy efficiency performance, reflecting its commitment to climate action and leadership in implementing environmental policies. Countries such as Norway and Sweden lead globally in electric vehicle adoption, while Sweden and Finland also show the highest forest coverage rates. Together, these trends highlight Europe as the most climate-progressive region globally. Meanwhile, Australia stands out as a climate leader in the southern hemisphere, although its energy efficiency score remains moderate compared to top European performers.

From the depths of the oceans to the crowns of the forests, our planet tells a single story — one of rising challenges and enduring hope, where every action toward preservation shapes the climate future we still have the power to protect.

Conclusion

The data across global indicators paints a complex picture of our planet’s evolving climate. Asia, led by China, remains the largest greenhouse gas emitter, followed closely by the United States. Australia, though smaller in scale, is still a significant contributor. Encouragingly, Australia’s energy consumption from coal, natural gas, and oil remains comparatively lower than many industrial nations — a positive sign of gradual transition toward cleaner energy.

However, Australia also records one of the highest surface temperature increases globally — rising by approximately 2.5°C in 2025 — coupled with moderate humidity levels. The global sea level continues to rise steadily, signalling an urgent warning for coastal regions and ecosystems. These changes intensify the effects of global warming and climate instability, leading to more frequent flooding, extreme weather events, and loss of biodiversity.

In terms of precipitation, Australia, the United States, and Denmark are among the wettest regions, while Libya and Mauritania remain the driest. Europe leads in electric vehicle adoption and forest coverage, reflecting robust climate policies and sustainable growth. In contrast, China continues to struggle with poor air quality and high emissions, illustrating the trade-offs of rapid industrial expansion. Australia, while moderate in energy efficiency, stands at a pivotal point in balancing economic growth with environmental responsibility.

Overall, Australia is moving in a promising direction — taking measured steps toward cleaner energy, stronger climate policies, and sustainable innovation. Yet, the rising temperatures serve as a reminder that progress must accelerate to safeguard the future climate for generations to come.
